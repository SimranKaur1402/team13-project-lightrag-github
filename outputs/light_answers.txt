QUESTION: What are the diagnostic criteria for diabetes mellitus?
MODE: naive
ANSWER:
The diagnostic criteria for diabetes mellitus are defined based on blood glucose levels, and are categorized into different situations, as follows:

1. **Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) Test**:
   - Diabetes is diagnosed if the fasting plasma glucose level is **≥7.0 mmol/L (≥126 mg/dL)**.
   - Fasting is defined as no caloric intake for at least 8 hours.

2. **Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)**:
   - Diabetes is confirmed with a 2-hour plasma glucose level of **≥11.1 mmol/L (≥200 mg/dL)** following a 75 g oral glucose load.

3. **Random Blood Glucose Test**:
   - A casual or random blood glucose level of **≥11.1 mmol/L (≥200 mg/dL)**, combined with classical symptoms of hyperglycemia or hyperglycemic crisis, can also indicate diabetes.

4. **Glycated Hemoglobin (HbA1c)**:
   - An HbA1c level of **≥6.5%** (≥48 mmol/mol) indicates diabetes. This test should be performed in a laboratory using a method standardized to NGSP and certified by the Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT).

For all diagnostic tests except for the random blood glucose test, confirmation of the diagnosis should be performed on a separate day unless the patient presents with unequivocal hyperglycemia and classic symptoms of diabetes.

These criteria ensure that diabetes is diagnosed accurately, preventing false positives that could lead to unnecessary anxiety and treatment.

QUESTION: What are the major acute and chronic complications of diabetes?

ANSWER:
Diabetes can lead to several significant acute and chronic complications due to prolonged periods of high blood glucose levels. Here’s a detailed overview of these complications:

### Acute Complications

1. **Hypoglycemia**: 
   - Occurs when blood glucose levels drop too low due to insufficient food intake, excessive exercise, or overdose of insulin or oral hypoglycemic agents. Symptoms may include trembling, sweating, confusion, and even loss of consciousness.

2. **Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)**: 
   - Primarily affects individuals with type 1 diabetes. Characterized by a buildup of ketones in the blood, it can present with nausea, vomiting, dry skin, deep rapid breathing, and low blood pressure. DKA can be life-threatening without prompt treatment.

3. **Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS)**: 
   - More common in type 2 diabetes, it typically occurs in the presence of severe infections or other acute illnesses, leading to dehydration and altered mental status. Unlike DKA, acidosis is uncommon.

4. **Infections**: 
   - Diabetes can impair the immune response, increasing susceptibility to various infections, including bacterial, fungal, and mycobacterial infections. Urinary tract infections are more common after bladder instrumentation.

### Chronic Complications

1. **Diabetic Retinopathy**: 
   - This eye disease is a leading cause of blindness, particularly affecting those with longer-standing diabetes. Almost all patients with type 1 diabetes will develop some form of retinopathy after 20 years, while 60% of individuals with type 2 diabetes may also be affected.

2. **Diabetic Nephropathy**: 
   - A major cause of renal failure, it significantly increases the risk of cardiovascular disease. Patients exhibit progressive albuminuria and a decline in kidney function over time.

3. **Diabetic Neuropathy**: 
   - It involves damage to the peripheral and autonomic nerves, leading to pain, loss of sensation, and other complications like impotence and gastrointestinal issues. The most common type is distal symmetrical sensory-motor polyneuropathy.

4. **Cardiovascular Complications**: 
   - Diabetes significantly raises the risk of atherosclerosis and cardiovascular diseases, such as coronary artery disease, cerebrovascular disease, and peripheral artery disease. Patients with diabetes often have an increased risk of heart attacks and strokes.

5. **Diabetic Foot Problems**: 
   - Neuropathy and poor blood circulation can lead to foot ulcers, infections, and, in severe cases, amputations. Regular foot examination and care are essential in managing diabetes.

6. **Skin Complications**: 
   - Diabetes can lead to various skin problems, including bacterial and fungal infections, and conditions like diabetic dermopathy.

7. **Gum Disease and Oral Health Issues**: 
   - Individuals with diabetes are at higher risk for gum disease, which can affect overall health.

Overall, these complications underscore the importance of managing blood glucose levels, blood pressure, and cholesterol to reduce the risk of both acute and chronic health issues associated with diabetes. Proper screening and regular check-ups can help in early detection and management of these complications.

QUESTION: What are the diagnostic criteria for diabetes mellitus?

ANSWER:
The diagnostic criteria for diabetes mellitus include the following parameters based on glucose levels:

1. **Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG):**
   - Diabetes mellitus is diagnosed when the FPG is ≥ 7.0 mmol/L (≥ 126 mg/dL).
   - A FPG of < 5.6 mmol/L (< 100 mg/dL) suggests exclusion of diabetes.
   - Values between 5.6–6.9 mmol/L (100–125 mg/dL) indicate an increased risk, and further diagnostic testing is recommended.

2. **2-Hour Plasma Glucose During an Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT):**
   - An OGTT is performed using a 75 g oral glucose load, where a result of ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (≥ 200 mg/dL) confirms diabetes.

3. **Random (Casual) Plasma Glucose:**
   - A casual plasma glucose measurement of ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (> 200 mg/dL) is also diagnostic for diabetes if accompanied by classic symptoms of hyperglycemia or hyperglycemic crisis.

4. **Glycated Hemoglobin (HbA1c):**
   - A diagnostic HbA1c level of ≥ 6.5% (48 mmol/mol) can also indicate diabetes.

It is important to note that a single abnormal glucose value is insufficient to make a diagnosis; confirmation through repeat testing is recommended unless the patient presents with unequivocal hyperglycemia and classic symptoms. 

These criteria ensure accurate identification of individuals with diabetes and facilitate timely intervention to manage the condition effectively.

### Major Complications of Diabetes

Diabetes can lead to both acute and chronic complications, each posing significant health risks.

#### Acute Complications

1. **Hypoglycemia**:
   - This condition occurs when blood glucose levels drop too low, causing symptoms such as nervousness, shakiness, and confusion. Severe hypoglycemia can lead to loss of consciousness or seizures.

2. **Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)**:
   - Primarily affects individuals with type 1 diabetes. DKA is a serious condition where the body produces excess blood acids called ketones. It can lead to severe dehydration, low blood pressure, and potentially death if not treated promptly.

3. **Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS)**:
   - More common in type 2 diabetes, this state is characterized by extremely high blood sugar levels, leading to dehydration and changes in mental status. Unlike DKA, there is typically no significant ketone production.

4. **Infections**:
   - People with poorly controlled diabetes are at increased risk for bacterial, fungal, and mycobacterial infections. Common infections include urinary tract infections, especially post bladder instrumentation, and skin infections. Severe infections can lead to life-threatening conditions if not managed effectively.

#### Chronic Complications

1. **Microvascular Complications**:
   - **Diabetic Retinopathy**: This is the leading cause of blindness in adults, with a high risk of developing significant visual impairment over time.
   - **Diabetic Nephropathy**: Kidney disease arising from diabetes can progress to end-stage renal failure, necessitating dialysis or transplant.
   - **Diabetic Neuropathy**: Nerve damage can lead to sensations of pain or numbness, particularly in the feet, increasing the risk of ulcers and amputations.

2. **Macrovascular Complications**:
   - **Cardiovascular Disease**: Patients with diabetes have a much higher risk of coronary heart disease, stroke, and peripheral artery disease due to atherosclerosis. This includes severe complications leading to increased morbidity and mortality.
   - **Cerebrovascular Disease**: Increased incidence of strokes is documented in diabetic patients, compounded by high blood pressure and cholesterol levels.

3. **Foot Complications**:
   - The risk of foot ulcers and amputations is significantly elevated due to neuropathy and poor circulation. Regular skin inspection and foot care are crucial.

4. **Other Complications**:
   - Individuals may also experience problems related to poor wound healing and skin issues, along with increased risk of developing other systemic conditions due to the high cardiac and vascular nature of diabetes.

Effective management involves regular monitoring and immediate intervention to mitigate these complications. It is crucial to educate patients and healthcare workers on the signs, prevention, and management of both acute and chronic complications of diabetes.

   - Include plenty of dietary fiber, aiming for **around 50 g/day**, which can benefit glycaemic control and cardiovascular health [30].
   - For those with dyslipidaemia, prioritize a diet that reduces saturated fat and cholesterol intake. This can help improve lipid profiles and reduce cardiovascular risk factors associated with diabetes [30].

2. **Weight Management**:
   - For individuals with a body mass index (BMI) of 25 kg/m² or greater, weight loss should be a significant objective as it can improve insulin sensitivity and overall diabetes control [30].

3. **Physical Activity**:
   - Incorporate regular exercise into daily routines. Aim for at least **150 minutes of moderate physical activity per week**, which includes various exercises that promote cardiovascular health and weight control [30].

### Medical Treatment

1. **Medication Adherence**:
   - Ensure that the patient is consistent with their prescribed diabetes medications, whether insulin or oral agents such as metformin, sulfonylureas, or thiazolidinediones, according to their specific needs and medical advice [30].

2. **Monitoring**:
   - Encourage regular self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG), which is crucial for understanding how food, activity, and medications impact blood glucose levels [30].
   - Obtain HbA1c levels every three months to evaluate long-term glycaemic control, aiming for a target below 7.0% [23].

### Regular Check-ups

1. **Comprehensive Diabetes Care**:
   - Schedule regular follow-ups with a multidisciplinary team, including a physician, dietician, and diabetes educator, to adjust the treatment plan as needed and ensure comprehensive care that addresses all aspects of diabetes management [30].
  
2. **Screen for Complications**:
   - Conduct screening for diabetes-related complications such as retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy regularly, especially in patients with long-standing diabetes [30].

### Education and Support

1. **Diabetes Education**:
   - Participate in diabetes education programs to enhance knowledge and skills regarding diabetes management. Understanding the condition improves adherence to treatment and the ability to manage it effectively [30].

2. **Community Support**:
   - Encourage involvement in community support groups for people with diabetes, which can offer social support and shared experiences, enhancing coping strategies and motivation.

### Conclusion
Implementing these strategies can help improve glycaemic control, reduce the risk of complications, and enhance the overall quality of life for patients with diabetes. It’s essential to individualize the approach based on each patient’s unique circumstances and preferences.
