   - **Population-Based Surveys**: Implement regular population-based surveys to screen for diabetes using methods such as fasting plasma glucose (FPG) tests, Oral Glucose Tolerance Tests (OGTT), and the HbA1c test to increase detection rates.

   - Train healthcare providers on the importance of recognizing and reporting undiagnosed diabetes cases and using standardized testing protocols for diagnosis.

5. **Data Quality Assurance**:
   - Establish strict protocols for data collection to ensure the accuracy and reliability of results. This may include calibration of testing equipment and regular training for personnel.

Based on WHO guidelines for diabetes prevention and care, the following indicators should be prioritized in the national diabetes surveillance system:

1. **Diabetes Prevalence Rates**:
   - Measure the prevalence of diagnosed and undiagnosed diabetes in various demographic groups.

   - Assess the percentage of high-risk individuals who have been screened for diabetes.

1. **Preventing Escalation**: While screening addresses early detection, funds must also support the management of chronic complications, which have significant morbidity and mortality associated with them. Evidence shows that comprehensive care programs can reduce hospital admissions and related costs associated with complications by facilitating early intervention and continuous management [21]. ### Conclusion

To maximize long-term health outcomes, a country should allocate a substantial portion of its healthcare funding towards screening programs due to their role in preventing complications through early detection and intervention. However, it should also ensure that enough resources are allocated to the management of chronic complications, as the complications from diabetes can not only be very expensive if left unmanaged but also significantly hinder the overall health of the population.

3. **Cost Implications**: Initial costs for population-wide screening can be substantial due to setup for mass screening, training personnel, and ensuring follow-up care. **Opportunistic screening** reduces these costs and ensures more immediate use of existing health resources.

4. **Follow-up Care**: Both methods require mechanisms for follow-up and management of identified cases. In low-capacity health systems, opportunistic screening may facilitate easier routing of identified high-risk individuals into the existing care framework.

5. **Psychological and Social Factors**: Mass screening can lead to anxiety related to false positives, which can strain health systems. Opportunistic screening can mitigate these impacts by directly addressing those who seek care.

### Conclusion
Overall, for countries with varying health system capacities, **opportunistic screening** is generally more **cost-effective**, especially in settings with lower prevalence and limited resources. It allows for targeted testing and efficient use of healthcare resources. In contrast, **population-wide screening** can be employed effectively in regions with high prevalence and robust healthcare systems that are capable of managing the follow-up care requirements.
